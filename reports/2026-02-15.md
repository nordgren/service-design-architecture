## Summary

Tonight's research covered the practical methods and frameworks that operationalize design thinking. Two major frameworks dominate the field:

### Stanford d.school Five Stages
The most widely taught framework, developed at the Hasso Plattner Institute of Design:

1. **Empathize** — Research users through interviews, observation, immersion
2. **Define** — Synthesize findings into human-centered problem statements
3. **Ideate** — Generate diverse solutions through structured creativity
4. **Prototype** — Build quick, tangible representations to learn
5. **Test** — Gather real user feedback and iterate

Key principle: Non-linear and iterative—stages overlap and loop back frequently.

### British Design Council Double Diamond (2004)
Visualizes design as two connected divergent-convergent cycles:

- **First Diamond** ("Designing the Right Thing"): Discover → Define
- **Second Diamond** ("Designing the Thing Right"): Develop → Deliver

Originated from a 2003 initiative led by Richard Eisermann, with key contributions from Anna White and Chris Vanstone. Built on earlier divergence-convergence models from Béla H. Bánáthy (1996) and others.

### Key Tools Documented
- **Empathize:** Empathy maps, user interviews, observation/shadowing
- **Define:** Personas, "How Might We" statements, affinity diagrams
- **Ideate:** Mind mapping, Crazy 8s, Brainstorming, SCAMPER, Worst Possible Idea
- **Prototype:** Paper prototypes, storyboards, Wizard of Oz testing
- **Test:** Usability testing, A/B testing

### Framework Comparison
Compared d.school (5 stages), IDEO (3 phases: Inspiration/Ideation/Implementation), and NN/g (6 phases—adds explicit "Implement" stage). All conceptually aligned; differences are in emphasis and audience.
