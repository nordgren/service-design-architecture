## Research Summary

Tonight's research focused on **service blueprinting**, a foundational tool in service design that visualizes the complete service delivery ecosystem—from customer-facing interactions to behind-the-scenes processes and support systems.

### Key Findings

#### Historical Context
- **Originated 1984** by G. Lynn Shostack (banking executive) in Harvard Business Review
- Revolutionary for codifying intangible services into documentable, measurable systems
- Classic example: "Blueprint for a Corner Shoeshine"
- Evolved dramatically over 40+ years in both visual form and structural sophistication

#### Core Architecture: The Three Lines
Service blueprints are structured around three critical horizontal boundaries:

1. **Line of Interaction**: Separates customer actions from organizational responses
2. **Line of Visibility**: The defining feature—separates frontstage (visible to customer) from backstage (invisible)
3. **Line of Internal Interaction**: Distinguishes customer-facing employees from internal support teams

#### Five Swim Lanes (Top to Bottom)
1. **Physical Evidence**: Tangible cues (websites, signage, receipts, spaces, uniforms)
2. **Customer Actions**: Steps customers take throughout their journey
3. **Frontstage Actions**: Visible employee/technology interactions
4. **Backstage Actions**: Invisible employee actions supporting frontstage
5. **Support Processes**: Internal systems and infrastructure enabling service delivery

#### Differentiation: Blueprints vs. Journey Maps

**Journey Maps** = Part 1: What customers experience (customer-centric, emotional, front-stage focused)

**Service Blueprints** = Part 2: How organizations deliver that experience (organization-centric, operational, front + back + support)

Key insight: "Journey maps visualize the customer's perspective across touchpoints, while service blueprints go deeper, mapping the internal processes, systems, and roles that support those interactions."

#### Six-Step Building Process
Research documented a structured methodology:
1. Identify service process scope
2. Identify customer segment
3. Map from customer's POV **first** (critical: never start with internal processes)
4. Map contact employee/technology actions
5. Link to support functions
6. Add physical evidence at each step

#### When Blueprints Excel
- Complex omnichannel services
- Cross-functional coordination needs
- Identifying systemic root causes (not just symptoms)
- Optimizing internal processes when customer experience is already understood
- Designing technology-delivered or hybrid services
- Exposing organizational silos and dependencies

#### Strategic Benefits
1. **Visualization**: Makes the invisible visible across silos
2. **Alignment**: Common canvas for cross-functional teams
3. **Discovery**: "Treasure maps" exposing weak links and bottlenecks
4. **Optimization**: Identifies redundancies and reuse opportunities
5. **Prototyping**: Low-fidelity testing before building
6. **Bridge-building**: Connects departments around shared customer outcomes

### Notable Insights

#### The Service Quality Challenge
> "Services constitute over 60% of the world's GDP, yet managing service quality is harder than managing the quality of goods. While goods can be evaluated through tangible cues such as style, hardness, color, label, feel, package, and fit, few cues exist for services."

#### Root Cause Diagnosis
> "While customers are convinced that someone is treating them badly, managers think defiant employees are the source of the malfunction. Even though services can fail because of human incompetence, a lack of systematic methods for design and control is often the core of the problem."

#### Shostack's Vision
> "A blueprint encourages creativity, preemptive problem solving, and controlled implementation. It can reduce the potential for failure and enhance management's ability to think effectively about new services."
