## Executive Summary

Completed comprehensive analysis of the journey mapping tool landscape as of March 2026. The market has matured significantly from early visualization tools (2015-2020) into three distinct tiers: general-purpose collaboration platforms (Miro, FigJam, Mural), dedicated mapping tools (Smaply, UXPressia, Custellence), and journey management platforms (TheyDo). 

**Key finding:** The industry is experiencing a fundamental shift from journey mapping as workshop artifact creation to journey management as operational practice. This evolution mirrors broader CX maturity, with leading organizations treating journey maps as living systems of record integrated with analytics, project management, and research repositories.

## Key Findings

### 1. Market Segmentation (Three-Tier Structure)

**Tier 1: General-Purpose Collaboration Tools**
- **Players:** Miro (80M users), FigJam, Mural, Lucidchart
- **Use case:** Journey mapping as one of many capabilities
- **Pricing:** $5-18/user/month
- **Market position:** "Good enough" for 80% of journey mapping needs
- **Strength:** Low adoption friction (teams already using them), flexible
- **Weakness:** No journey-specific governance or operational features

**Tier 2: Dedicated Mapping Tools**
- **Players:** Smaply (€390-990/month), UXPressia ($160-360/month), Custellence ($1,999/month)
- **Use case:** Professional journey maps, personas, stakeholder maps
- **Market position:** Mid-market sweet spot, agencies, consultancies
- **Strength:** Purpose-built UI, professional outputs, template libraries
- **Weakness:** Limited operational/integration capabilities as maps scale

**Tier 3: Journey Management Platforms**
- **Players:** TheyDo ($35K/year starting)
- **Use case:** Journey-centric operating models, enterprise governance
- **Market position:** Mature CX programs (Level 3-4 maturity)
- **Strength:** Only platform treating journeys as operational artifacts, deep integrations (Qualtrics, Jira, Notion)
- **Weakness:** Enterprise pricing, steeper learning curve, requires organizational readiness

### 2. The Mapping → Management Evolution

Traditional journey mapping workflow (2015-2022):
1. Workshop creates map → 2. Export PDF/PNG → 3. Store in SharePoint → 4. Map goes stale → 5. Next workshop starts over

Modern journey management workflow (2023+):
1. Map created in platform → 2. Linked to research repository → 3. Connected to project tracking → 4. Updated as initiatives launch → 5. Analytics show actual vs. intended journey → 6. Becomes living operational artifact

**Implication:** Organizations at CX maturity level 3+ need platforms, not tools. But 80% of organizations still at level 1-2, making general tools or dedicated mappers appropriate.

### 3. AI Capabilities (Emerging Differentiator)

As of March 2026, AI features becoming table stakes:

**Leaders:**
- **TheyDo Journey AI:** Auto-converts research transcripts into structured maps with categorized insights (needs/pains/gains). Most sophisticated journey-specific AI.
- **FigJam:** Prompt-to-journey generation. Impressive but generic (not journey-domain trained).
- **Miro AI Canvas:** Sticky note generation, clustering, diagram creation. Helpful but not journey-specific.

**Laggards:**
- Custellence: No AI features
- Lucidchart: Basic AI diagramming

**Trend:** Research synthesis (transcript → insights → journey map) is where AI delivers real ROI. Generic AI features (image generation, text editing) less valuable.

### 4. Enterprise Selection Criteria

**Critical decision factors** (based on practitioner forums, reviews):

1. **Adoption over features:** Most-cited complaint: "We paid for [tool X] but teams still use PowerPoint." Cultural fit and ease of use matter more than feature count.

2. **Integration requirements:**
   - Low: Any tool works
   - Medium: Export/import (Smaply, UXPressia, Miro)
   - High: Bi-directional sync (TheyDo only platform with native Jira, Qualtrics, Notion integration)

3. **Scale considerations:**
   - <10 journeys/year: Free tiers or general tools
   - 10-50 journeys: Dedicated tools (Smaply, UXPressia)
   - 50+ journeys: Journey management platform (TheyDo) or heavy Miro customization

4. **Maturity alignment:**
   - Level 1 (ad-hoc): PowerPoint, Miro, FigJam
   - Level 2 (repeatable): Smaply, UXPressia, Custellence
   - Level 3 (managed): TheyDo or custom Miro governance
   - Level 4 (optimized): TheyDo + real-time analytics integration

### 5. Pricing Analysis

**Budget Bands:**
- **<$1K/year:** Free tiers (all), Miro Starter, FigJam ($5/seat)
- **$1K-10K/year:** Smaply Repository (€390/mo), UXPressia Pro ($360/mo), Miro Business for small team
- **$10K-50K/year:** TheyDo Management ($35K/year), Custellence Collaborate ($1,999/mo)
- **$50K+/year:** TheyDo Strategic (custom), indicates enterprise CX program

**Hidden costs:** Per-user models (Miro, FigJam, Mural) scale expensively; flat-rate models (Smaply, UXPressia) limit features on lower tiers. Total cost of ownership requires factoring in change management and integration maintenance.

### 6. Tool-Specific Insights

**Miro:** 800-pound gorilla (80M users). Journey mapping one of hundreds of use cases. Strength is ecosystem (160+ integrations) and flexibility. Weakness: no journey governance features, maps become static artifacts unless custom process built. Recommendation: Use if team already has licenses; don't buy specifically for journey mapping unless budget-constrained.

**FigJam:** Lightweight Figma whiteboard. Best for design teams who want journey mapping integrated with UI/UX workflow. $5/seat flat pricing attractive. Limitation: narrower template library than Miro, no advanced journey features. Recommendation: Ideal for design-centric teams transitioning brainstorm → wireframe → prototype.

**Smaply:** Pioneer in dedicated journey mapping (Austrian/German heritage, strong in Europe). Intuitive drag-and-drop editor, pain point cards, stakeholder mapping. Limitation: dated interface, no undo/redo (frequent complaint), limited data integration. Recommendation: Good for mid-market teams, affordable entry (€390/mo), but may need migration as practice matures.

**UXPressia:** Comprehensive CX platform (journey maps + personas + impact maps). 100+ templates, strong customization (20+ section types). Popular in agencies/consulting for client deliverables. Limitation: only 3 concurrent maps on Starter tier, higher per-user cost than competitors. Recommendation: Best for professional outputs and agencies billing clients.

**Custellence:** Differentiates on non-grid structure (maximum creative freedom). SOC2-certified (enterprise security). Unique pricing: $0 free tier, then jumps to $1,999/month. Limitation: cannot create personas within maps, no map ownership transfer. Recommendation: For CX experts who need custom layouts, but steep price jump limits mid-market appeal.

**TheyDo:** Only true journey management platform (vs. mapping tool). Built for journey-centric operating models. Journey AI for research synthesis, deep integrations (Qualtrics, Jira, Notion, Airtable), custom taxonomy, governance controls. Starts at $35K/year. Limitation: requires organizational maturity (Level 3+), not for episodic mapping. Recommendation: Essential for enterprises managing 50+ journeys across departments; overkill for workshop-focused teams.

**Mural:** Workshop-focused collaboration tool (timers, voting, frameworks). Strong facilitation features for design sprints. Journey mapping supported but not core focus. Recommendation: Use if running frequent design thinking workshops; maps remain workshop artifacts, not operational tools.

**Lucidchart:** Diagramming tool that happens to support journey maps. Strong on process integration (org charts, flowcharts, network diagrams). 100+ integrations with Atlassian/Microsoft ecosystems. Recommendation: Good when journeys need to link to process maps; otherwise overkill with diagramming paradigm unfamiliar to non-technical teams.

### 7. Regional & Industry Variations

**Geographic:**
- North America: Miro dominant, TheyDo gaining Fortune 500 traction
- Europe: Smaply strong (Austrian heritage), GDPR compliance emphasized
- Asia-Pacific: Miro/FigJam lead due to English support, local alternatives emerging

**Industry-specific:**
- Financial services: Lucidchart or TheyDo (governance, security)
- Healthcare: HIPAA compliance narrows options, persona features valued
- Retail/E-commerce: Data integration critical (Google Analytics, Shopify)
- B2B SaaS: Product analytics integration (Mixpanel, Amplitude, Pendo)
- Public sector: Budget constraints favor free/low-cost (Miro, Lucidchart)

### 8. Common Selection Mistakes

**Mistake 1:** Over-buying for current maturity (small team buys TheyDo at $35K/year for 2 maps/quarter)  
**Mistake 2:** Under-buying for scale ambitions (large org standardizes on Smaply, hits limits at 100+ journeys)  
**Mistake 3:** Choosing based on demos, not pilots (tool looks great in demo, actual workflow cumbersome)  
**Mistake 4:** Ignoring change management (sophisticated platform, but frontline teams won't use it)  
**Mistake 5:** Betting on single tool category (assuming Miro scales to journey management without custom governance)

### 9. Future Outlook (2026-2028)

**Predicted developments:**

1. **AI becomes table stakes (2026-2027):** All major platforms will add AI research synthesis. Differentiation shifts from "do you have AI?" to "how good is your AI?"

2. **Real-time journey analytics matures (2027):** Deep integration with CDPs (Customer Data Platforms) and product analytics. Journey maps overlay live customer behavior, gap analysis (intended vs. actual) automated.

3. **Journey orchestration convergence (2027-2028):** Journey mapping platforms merge with journey orchestration tools (Adobe Journey Optimizer, Salesforce Marketing Cloud). Design + execution become unified workflow.

4. **Consolidation in dedicated tool segment (2026-2027):** Market can't support 5-10 dedicated tools long-term. Expect M&A (e.g., Qualtrics acquires Smaply, Medallia acquires UXPressia). Survivors: TheyDo (platform play) and 1-2 mid-market tools.

5. **Miro/FigJam maintain majority (ongoing):** Despite limitations, general-purpose tools will remain most common choice. 80% of journey maps created in Miro/FigJam, 20% in dedicated platforms (Pareto principle).

## Practical Recommendations

### Decision Framework (Simplified)

**If you're doing episodic journey mapping (workshops, quarterly):**
→ Use what your team already knows (Miro/FigJam), or pick dedicated tool for visual quality (Smaply/UXPressia)

**If you're running a journey-centric program (ongoing, operational):**
→ <50 journeys: Smaply or UXPressia  
→ 50+ journeys: TheyDo or heavy Miro customization

**If your organization is at CX maturity level 3+ (managed practice with dedicated resources):**
→ Yes: Journey management platform (TheyDo)  
→ No: Dedicated mapping tool or general-purpose tool

### Scenario-Specific Recommendations

**Small Product Team (5-15 people, quarterly workshops):**
- 1st choice: Miro Free or FigJam Free
- 2nd choice: Smaply Free
- Avoid: TheyDo, Custellence (over-investment)

**CX Team in Mid-Sized Company (50-200 employees, 10-20 maps/year):**
- 1st choice: Smaply Repository (€390/mo) or UXPressia Pro ($360/mo)
- 2nd choice: Miro Business + governance process (if already licensed)
- Avoid: TheyDo (too expensive unless scaling to 50+ journeys)

**Enterprise CX Program (500+ employees, 100+ journeys, governance needs):**
- 1st choice: TheyDo Management or Strategic
- 2nd choice: Miro Enterprise + Confluence + custom governance (DIY alternative)
- Avoid: Mid-market tools (won't scale)

**Consultancy/Agency (client deliverables, fast turnaround):**
- 1st choice: UXPressia Pro ($360/mo) — best templates, professional exports
- 2nd choice: Smaply Framework (€690/mo)
- Avoid: General tools unless clients already using them

**Design Team in Tech Company (journey → wireframes → prototypes):**
- 1st choice: FigJam Professional ($5/seat/mo) — native Figma integration
- 2nd choice: Miro Business (if using Jira/Confluence more than Figma)
- Avoid: Dedicated journey tools (adds overhead)

## Meta-Insights

**The lesson:** Tool choice matters less than tool usage. A team fully adopting Miro with clear governance beats a team half-heartedly using TheyDo. Start simple, scale as maturity grows, and be willing to migrate when you outgrow your tools.

**The pattern:** Organizations progress through predictable stages:
1. Ad-hoc (PowerPoint/Miro)
2. Repeatable (Dedicated mapping tool)
3. Managed (Journey management platform)
4. Optimized (Data-driven journey operations)

Tools designed for one stage don't necessarily scale to the next, creating transition costs and change management challenges. This explains why no single tool dominates the market—different organizations at different maturity levels need different capabilities.

**The paradox:** Despite all this sophistication, most journey maps are still created in PowerPoint or Miro. The market for advanced journey management platforms is smaller than vendor hype suggests, because most organizations remain at maturity level 1-2 (ad-hoc or repeatable). TheyDo and similar platforms are building for a future state that only the most mature CX programs have reached.

## Implications for Service Design × Business Architecture Practice

**For Mattias's consulting/advisory work:**

1. **Tool recommendations require maturity assessment first:** Don't recommend TheyDo to a client at maturity level 1-2, even if budget allows. They'll fail on adoption, not capability.

2. **Journey governance is the hard part:** Any organization can buy Smaply or Miro. Few organizations can establish the process discipline to maintain journey maps as operational artifacts. The tool is 20% of the solution; governance design is 80%.

3. **Integration architecture matters:** If a client is standardizing on Qualtrics for research, Jira for projects, and Confluence for documentation, tool selection should account for integration overhead. TheyDo's native integrations reduce friction; Miro requires custom bridging.

4. **Platform migration is inevitable for mature programs:** Organizations that succeed at journey management eventually outgrow their first tool. Factor migration costs and timing into multi-year roadmaps.

5. **The Miro default:** When in doubt, Miro is the safe choice for most organizations. Not because it's the best journey mapping tool, but because it's already in use, procurement is easy, and it's "good enough" for 80% of needs. Only recommend specialized tools when you can articulate clear ROI.
