## Summary

Tonight's research explored the origins of Service Design as a formal discipline, tracing its emergence from the 1980s banking sector through its evolution into a globally recognized practice. The research focused heavily on G. Lynn Shostack's pioneering work and the academic developments that followed in Europe.

## Key Findings

### 1. **G. Lynn Shostack: The Founding Figure**
- **Background**: Banking executive at Citibank who recognized services needed systematic design
- **1982**: Coined the term "Service Design" in *European Journal of Marketing*
- **1984**: Published landmark HBR article "Designing Services That Deliver" introducing service blueprinting
- **Innovation**: Created visual methodology to map both visible (frontstage) and invisible (backstage) service processes
- **Impact**: Her "Line of Visibility" concept remains fundamental 40+ years later

### 2. **Service Blueprinting Methodology**
The service blueprint proved revolutionary because it:
- Made invisible processes visible and manageable
- Enabled systematic diagnosis of operational problems
- Provided a planning tool for service innovation
- Bridged the gap between marketing (customer view) and operations (delivery systems)

Core components established in 1984:
- Customer actions
- Frontstage/backstage employee actions
- Support processes
- Physical evidence
- Line of visibility

### 3. **Historical Context: The Service Economy Shift**
- **1950s-1960s**: Economies shifting from manufacturing to services; early customer-centric marketing
- **1970s**: Recognition that services fundamentally differ from products (intangibility, inseparability, variability, perishability)
- **1980s**: Formalization era—service design gains theoretical foundation
- **Late 1980s**: IBM research showed 15% reduction in friction points through journey mapping

### 4. **Academic Development: The European Lead**
**Köln International School of Design (KISD)**
- **1991**: First formal service design education program globally
- **1995**: Birgit Mager establishes first worldwide professorship in Service Design
- Interdisciplinary approach: design + business + psychology + engineering
- Trained the first generation of professional service designers

**Birgit Mager: "The Mother of Service Design"**
- Teaching/researching since 1995 (30+ years)
- Co-founded Service Design Network (2004) with Shelley Evenson
- Editor-in-Chief of *Touchpoint* journal
- Recognized that pioneers felt isolated until ~2006 when critical mass emerged

### 5. **1990s: Migration from Marketing to Design**
- Service design began in marketing/management departments
- Design schools adopted it as human-centered design thinking spread
- IDEO's human-centered methods showed 25% satisfaction improvements
- Toyota case studies demonstrated 20% efficiency gains
- Shift from backend operations focus to holistic experience design

### 6. **Early 2000s: Professionalization**
- **Specialized consultancies emerge**: Livework (2001), Engine (2000)
- **30% annual growth** in service design agencies by 2005
- **Standardized methodologies**: Journey maps, personas, prototyping become industry standard
- **Nielsen Norman Group (2004)**: Organizations using these methods saw 40% retention increase
- Service design recognized as distinct from UX, product design, and business consulting

### 7. **Dual Heritage**
Service design has always been interdisciplinary:
- **From marketing/management**: Service quality frameworks, operational efficiency, customer satisfaction metrics
- **From design**: Visual thinking, prototyping, iteration, human-centered approaches, systems thinking
- **Unique synthesis**: Neither purely analytical nor purely creative—integrates both

### 8. **Geographic Spread**
- **Origins**: USA (Shostack's banking work)
- **Academic leadership**: Europe (Germany, UK, Italy strongest)
- **Commercial pioneers**: UK consultancies
- **2004**: Service Design Network provides global coordination
- **2006**: Recognized globally as a movement

### 9. **Enduring Concepts**
From the 1980s origins that remain foundational today:
- **Services are designable**: Not left to chance or individual talent
- **Frontstage/backstage dichotomy**: What customers see vs. what enables delivery
- **Complexity and divergence**: Two dimensions for positioning services (number of steps vs. customization latitude)
- **The Line of Visibility**: The boundary that shapes service perception and operational management
- **Blueprint as multi-purpose tool**: Representation, diagnosis, and planning

### 10. **Critical Perspectives**
The research also surfaced important critiques:
- **Service Design ≠ Service Marketing**: Emphasizes design of delivery systems, not just promotional strategy
- **Beyond "Services as Products"**: Recognition of co-creation, temporality, contextuality
- **The "Engineering" Critique**: Risk of over-standardization; but blueprints enable consistency *and* discretion
