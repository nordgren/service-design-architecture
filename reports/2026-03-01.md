## Research Summary

Tonight's research explored how organizations assign accountability for end-to-end customer journeys that cross functional boundaries. This is a critical governance challenge in Journey Ops: journeys don't map cleanly to org charts, requiring new ownership models that bridge silos.

### Key Themes Discovered

1. **Journey Manager as Role**
   - Parallel to product management: owns the journey like a PM owns a product
   - One manager per journey (not per multiple journeys)
   - Requires high organizational placement with cross-functional authority
   - Responsibilities: strategic vision, continuous optimization, stakeholder coordination

2. **Ownership Model Patterns**
   - **Single Executive** (CCO, CMO, CEO, COO): Clear accountability but function-dependent blind spots
   - **Multiple Stakeholder** (46% "extremely positive" rating): Shared accountability across functions
   - **Dedicated Teams**: Specialized journey-management teams with data scientists, researchers, designers

3. **Journey Centers of Excellence (CoE)**
   - Centralized capability for standards, governance, training, and coordination
   - Three operating models: centralized, federated, hybrid
   - Critical for scaling journey management across multiple journeys

4. **Four-Part Governance Framework** (Smaply)
   - **Ownership & Roles**: Named owner + contributors + stakeholders
   - **Review Cadence**: Quarterly scheduled + trigger-based reviews
   - **Standards & Quality**: Format consistency, data requirements, versioning
   - **Connection to Action**: Prioritization, traceability, metrics integration

5. **Journey vs. Process Ownership**
   - Fundamental shift from inside-out (process) to outside-in (journey)
   - Process = internal efficiency; Journey = customer experience
   - Process = single function; Journey = cross-functional

6. **Maturity Evolution**
   - Level 1 (Ad Hoc): No formal ownership
   - Level 2 (Emerging): Owners named but lack resources
   - Level 3 (Defined): Clear owners with standards
   - Level 4 (Managed): CoE established, portfolio view
   - Level 5 (Optimizing): Orchestration platforms, embedded in operating model

7. **Minimum Viable Governance**
   - Start small: 1 owner + quarterly review + update log + 1 metric per stage + accessible maps
   - Time investment: <1 day setup, <2 hours/quarter maintenance
   - Prove value for one journey before scaling

### Emerging Concepts

**Journey Atlas**: Portfolio-level framework for managing multiple journeys at scale, providing inventory, prioritization, interdependencies, and metrics dashboard.

**DACI for Journey Decisions**: Driver (journey owner), Approver (exec sponsor), Contributors (all functions), Informed (adjacent teams). Prevents "highest-paid opinion" deadlocks.

**Journey vs. Product**: Organizational shift from product managers to journey managers (example: Optus telecom).

**Cross-Journey Coordination**: Identifying shared pain points and overlapping touchpoints across journeys to prevent duplicate efforts.

## Key Insights for Journey Ops Practice

1. **Ownership must be singular** even when accountability is shared. Multiple stakeholders contribute, but one person must be accountable.

2. **Governance is operational, not strategic**. It's the system that keeps maps alive between creation and use. Without governance, even excellent maps become stale documentation.

3. **Connection to action is non-negotiable**. Well-maintained maps that don't drive decisions are expensive decoration. Governance must feed prioritization.

4. **Start small and prove value**. Don't over-engineer governance before you have journeys worth governing. Begin with minimum viable governance for one journey.

5. **Journey ownership requires organizational design**. Journey managers need scope of influence, authority to convene stakeholders, and executive support.

6. **CoEs enable scaling**. Without centralized standards and coordination, journey management fragments into inconsistent practices.
