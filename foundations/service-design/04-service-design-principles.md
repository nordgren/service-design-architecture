# Service Design Principles

*Research completed: 2026-02-18*

## Overview

Service design principles provide the foundational mindset and approach for creating services that deliver value to users while remaining efficient and effective for organizations. These principles emerged from the formalization of service design as a discipline, particularly through the influential work of Marc Stickdorn and Jakob Schneider's 2011 book "This is Service Design Thinking."

## The Five Core Principles (Stickdorn & Schneider, 2011)

### 1. User-Centered (Human-Centered)

**Definition:** Services should be designed based on a genuine comprehension of all users affected by the service, not just end consumers.

**Key aspects:**
- Focus on understanding users' perspectives, needs, and behaviors
- Consider ALL users: customers, front-line staff, managers, third-party vendors
- Base design on deep understanding of psychological and behavioral drivers
- Design services from the outside-in, not inside-out

**Quote:** "Services should be designed based on customer needs rather than the internal needs of the business." - Design4Services

### 2. Co-Creative

**Definition:** Service design is a collaborative process that involves various stakeholders, including end-users, in the design process.

**Key aspects:**
- Engage all stakeholders and subject matter experts in co-design
- Ensure all voices are heard across the organization
- Create solutions considering diverse needs and viewpoints
- Foster collaboration for better alignment, cohesion, and innovation
- Leads to more practical and accepted solutions

**Implication:** Co-creation ensures that services aren't designed in isolation but reflect the collective intelligence of everyone involved in delivery and use.

### 3. Sequencing

**Definition:** Services should be visualized as a sequence of interrelated actions, processes, and touchpoints over time.

**Key aspects:**
- Break down complex services into logical, visual, and rhythmic sequences
- Visualize through service blueprints or customer journey maps
- Map step-by-step from first interaction to last
- Identify pain points, opportunities, and critical touchpoints
- Treat services as interconnected processes and events that impact overall perception

**Tools:** Service blueprints, customer journey maps, experience maps

### 4. Evidencing

**Definition:** Intangible services should be made tangible through physical or digital artifacts.

**Key aspects:**
- Make the invisible visible
- Create tangible elements that represent the service experience
- Help customers understand, evaluate, and appreciate the service
- Add concrete touchpoints to perishable experiences
- Drive customer loyalty through memorable artifacts

**Examples:**
- Boarding pass for air travel service
- App interface for online banking
- Receipt or confirmation email
- Physical brand environments

### 5. Holistic

**Definition:** Service design considers the entire environment in which a service exists, is delivered, and experienced.

**Key aspects:**
- View the service as part of a larger ecosystem
- Consider all related components and touchpoints
- Look beyond individual interactions to the complete experience
- Ensure cohesive and consistent service delivery
- Consider cultural, technological, and economic factors
- Account for the entire user journey, not just the product

**Quote:** "Genuinely working in a holistic way is an illusion, it is simply impossible to consider every single aspect — but it's the mindset that matters." - Stickdorn & Schneider

## Additional Design4Services Principles

Design4Services, an organization committed to developing service design practice, articulated complementary principles across different design domains:

### General Service Design Principles

- Design based on genuine comprehension of purpose, demand, and delivery capability
- Focus on customer needs over internal business needs
- Design for unified, efficient systems rather than component-by-component
- Create value for users while maximizing efficiency
- Treat special events as common events (design for variation)
- Always include user input
- Prototype before full development
- Base services on clear business case and model
- Deploy as Minimum Viable Service (MVS) and iterate
- Collaborate with all relevant stakeholders

### Process Design Principles

- Eliminate or minimize activities that don't add customer value
- Structure work around processes, not internal constructs
- Keep work integrated (avoid fragmentation)
- Simplify processes: reduce steps, handovers, rules, controls
- Reflect customer needs (accept multiple process versions)
- Minimize process variation
- Minimize process dependencies (parallelize where possible)
- Internalize processes (training over work instructions)
- Minimize breaks, delays, reconciliation, and controls
- Measure only what matters

### Organizational Design Principles

- Organize work groups to match processes and required competencies
- Give workers sufficient autonomy for decision-making
- Work happens where it's most efficient

### Information Design Principles

- Normalize data between organization and customers
- Make data easy to transfer and reusable
- Replace data entry with lookup, selection, and confirmation utilities

### Technology Design Principles

- Technology enables service; it should not drive it
- Technology should be pulled into service design, not pushed
- Design for flexibility and agility to accommodate changing requirements

## Historical Context

**Origins:**
- Service Design established as academic field in 1991 by Michael Erlhoff and Birgit Mager at Köln International School of Design (KISD)
- 2009: Stefan Moritz published "Service Design: Practical Access to an Evolving Field"
- 2011: Marc Stickdorn and Jakob Schneider published "This is Service Design Thinking: Basics, Tools, Cases"
- 2010s: Widespread adoption across commercial and public sectors

**Evolution:**
The five principles represent a distillation of design thinking applied to services, emphasizing the shift from product-centric to service-centric thinking.

## Related Frameworks & Methods

### Customer Journey Mapping
Visual representation of all customer interactions with a brand across touchpoints, identifying barriers and critical moments.

### Service Blueprinting
Elevated form of customer journey maps that shows the relationship between:
- Customer actions (frontstage)
- Employee actions (backstage)
- Support processes
- Physical/digital evidence
- Line of visibility and interaction

### Personas
Fictional, generalized representations of target users based on research, including demographics, behaviors, needs, and goals.

## Notable Practitioners & Thought Leaders

### Marc Stickdorn
Co-author of "This is Service Design Thinking" (2011) and "This is Service Design Doing" (2017). Founder of Smaply service design software.

**Key quote:** "Firstly, viewing your service through the customers' eyes, and secondly, designing in such a way that customers receive consistent experiences over time which they consider valuable."

### Jakob Schneider
Co-author with Stickdorn, contributed to establishing the five core principles framework.

### Stefan Moritz
Early service design educator and author who helped formalize the discipline. Defined service design as helping to "innovate (create new) or improve (existing) services to make them more useful, usable, desirable for clients and efficient as well as effective for organisations."

### Birgit Mager & Michael Erlhoff
Founders of service design as an academic discipline at KISD in 1991.

## Contemporary Applications

Service design principles are now applied across:
- Healthcare (chronic disease management, patient experience)
- Financial services (banking, insurance)
- Retail and hospitality
- Public services and government
- Technology platforms and digital services
- Transportation and mobility

**Key insight:** "When you have two coffee shops right next to each other, and each sells the exact same coffee at the exact same price, service design is what makes you walk into one and not the other."

## Critical Perspective

While the five principles provide an excellent starting framework, service design practitioners note:

- True holistic thinking is aspirational — it's about the mindset, not achieving perfection
- Principles must be adapted to context (B2B vs B2C, digital vs physical)
- Co-creation requires genuine commitment, not token involvement
- Evidencing must be authentic to brand values, not just surface decoration
- Sequencing can over-simplify non-linear service experiences

## Key Takeaway

Service design principles shift the focus from creating isolated touchpoints to orchestrating comprehensive, meaningful experiences. They emphasize understanding the human experience, collaborating across boundaries, visualizing the invisible, and thinking systemically about service ecosystems.
