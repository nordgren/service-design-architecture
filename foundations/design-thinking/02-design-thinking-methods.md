# Design Thinking Methods

*Research compiled: 2026-02-15*

## Overview

Design thinking methods provide structured yet flexible frameworks for human-centered problem solving. This document covers the two most influential frameworks—the Stanford d.school Five Stages and the British Design Council's Double Diamond—along with key techniques and tools used across design thinking practice.

---

## The Stanford d.school Five Stages

The Hasso Plattner Institute of Design at Stanford (d.school) developed the most widely recognized design thinking framework, consisting of five iterative phases.

### Stage 1: Empathize

**Purpose:** Develop deep understanding of users and their needs through research.

**Activities:**
- Conduct user interviews and observations
- Immerse yourself in users' physical environments
- Consult domain experts
- Practice "beginner's mind"—setting aside assumptions

**Key insight:** Empathy allows design thinkers to set aside their own assumptions about the world and gain real insight into users' actual needs and motivations.

### Stage 2: Define

**Purpose:** Synthesize empathy research into a clear, human-centered problem statement.

**Activities:**
- Analyze and organize observations from empathy phase
- Identify patterns and themes
- Create "How Might We" statements
- Develop a point of view based on user needs

**Example transformation:**
- ❌ Company-centric: "We need to increase market share among teenage girls by 5%"
- ✅ Human-centric: "Teenage girls need to eat nutritious food in order to thrive, be healthy and grow"

### Stage 3: Ideate

**Purpose:** Generate a wide range of creative solutions to the defined problem.

**Key techniques:**
- **Brainstorming** — free-flowing idea generation
- **Brainwriting** — silent individual ideation followed by sharing
- **Worst Possible Idea** — generate deliberately bad ideas to break creative blocks
- **SCAMPER** — Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to other uses, Eliminate, Reverse

**Principle:** Quantity over quality initially; defer judgment to expand the solution space.

### Stage 4: Prototype

**Purpose:** Create tangible representations of ideas to explore and test.

**Characteristics:**
- Inexpensive, scaled-down versions
- "Build to think"—learning through making
- Quick and iterative
- Focus on key features, not complete solutions

**Types:** Paper prototypes, wireframes, storyboards, physical mockups, role-playing scenarios.

### Stage 5: Test

**Purpose:** Gather feedback and learn from real user interactions with prototypes.

**Activities:**
- Put prototypes in front of real users
- Observe behavior, not just stated preferences
- Ask "Does this solve the problem?" and "How does this change how users feel/think/do?"
- Iterate based on learnings

**Key insight:** Testing often reveals needs to loop back to earlier stages—this is expected and valuable.

## The Double Diamond Framework

### Origins

Developed by the British Design Council in 2004, the Double Diamond emerged from a collaborative effort to standardize how design process was described.

**Key figures:**
- Richard Eisermann (Director of Design and Innovation, 2003)
- Anna White (ergonomist, product designer)
- Chris Vanstone (product designer, service design pioneer)
- Gill Wildman, Jennie Winhall, Jonathan Ball

**Intellectual lineage:** Built on divergence-convergence models from Herbert Simon, Béla H. Bánáthy (1996), Nigel Cross, and others dating back to the 1960s.

### The 4D Framework

The Double Diamond visualizes design as two connected diamonds, each representing a divergent-convergent cycle:

```
    DISCOVER          DEFINE          DEVELOP          DELIVER
      ╱  ╲              ╱  ╲              ╱  ╲              ╱  ╲
     ╱    ╲            ╱    ╲            ╱    ╲            ╱    ╲
    ╱      ╲          ╱      ╲          ╱      ╲          ╱      ╲
   ╱        ╲        ╱        ╲        ╱        ╲        ╱        ╲
  ╱__________╲______╱__________╲______╱__________╲______╱__________╲
  
  Challenge       Problem Definition              Solution
```

### First Diamond: "Designing the Right Thing"

**Discover (Divergent)**
- Question the initial challenge
- Research to identify user needs
- Explore the problem space broadly
- Gather diverse perspectives

**Define (Convergent)**
- Make sense of findings
- Understand how user needs and problem align
- Create a clear design brief
- Focus on the right problem to solve

### Second Diamond: "Designing the Thing Right"

**Develop (Divergent)**
- Generate multiple potential solutions
- Prototype and test ideas
- Explore different approaches
- Encourage experimentation

**Deliver (Convergent)**
- Select the best solution
- Refine and finalize
- Prepare for launch/implementation
- Focus on execution quality

### Divergent vs Convergent Thinking

| Divergent Thinking | Convergent Thinking |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| Exploring widely | Focusing narrowly |
| Generating options | Selecting options |
| "Opening up" | "Closing down" |
| Quantity of ideas | Quality of execution |
| Breadth | Depth |

## Common Design Thinking Tools

### For Empathize Phase

**Empathy Maps**
- Four quadrants: Says, Thinks, Does, Feels
- Synthesizes user research into actionable insights
- Helps teams build shared understanding

**User Interviews**
- Open-ended questions
- Focus on stories and experiences
- Look for emotions and motivations

**Observation/Shadowing**
- Watch users in their natural context
- Note workarounds and pain points
- Don't interrupt—observe authentically

### For Define Phase

**Personas**
- Fictional representations of user archetypes
- Include goals, frustrations, behaviors
- Keep research-grounded, not imaginary

**How Might We Statements**
- Transform problems into opportunities
- Format: "How might we [verb] [user need] so that [outcome]?"
- Balance: not too broad, not too narrow

**Affinity Diagrams**
- Cluster related observations
- Find themes and patterns
- Collaborative synthesis tool

### For Ideate Phase

**Mind Mapping**
- Visual brainstorming
- Branch ideas from central concept
- Tools: MindMeister, Miro, paper

**Crazy 8s**
- Fold paper into 8 sections
- Sketch 8 ideas in 8 minutes
- Rapid, quantity-focused

**Dot Voting**
- Democratize idea selection
- Quick prioritization
- Visual consensus building

### For Prototype Phase

**Paper Prototypes**
- Sketched interfaces
- Low-fidelity, high iteration speed
- Test flow and concept before building

**Storyboards**
- Sequential illustrations of user journey
- Show context and emotional arc
- Communicate the full experience

**Wizard of Oz Prototypes**
- Human simulates system functionality
- Test without building
- Validate concept before investment

### For Test Phase

**Usability Testing**
- Task-based evaluation with real users
- Think-aloud protocol
- Measure success, efficiency, satisfaction

**A/B Testing**
- Compare solution variants
- Data-driven decisions
- Scale validation beyond qualitative

## Framework Comparison: d.school vs IDEO vs NNg

| Aspect | d.school (5 stages) | IDEO (3 phases) | NN/g (6 phases) |
|--------|-------------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Structure | Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test | Inspiration, Ideation, Implementation | Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test, Implement |
| Focus | Learning/educational | Business/consulting | UX/practical |
| Unique element | Strong empathy emphasis | Implementation focus | Adds explicit implement phase |
| Iteration | Non-linear, loops back | Cyclical | Flexible, iterative |

**Note:** These frameworks are conceptually aligned—the differences are primarily in emphasis and terminology. IDEO (where the term "design thinking" was coined by David Kelley and Tim Brown) adds explicit focus on implementation. The Nielsen Norman Group explicitly adds a sixth "Implement" phase, noting that "design thinking does not free you from the actual design doing."

## The Non-Linear Nature of Design Thinking

A critical insight: design thinking is **not** a linear, step-by-step process.

### How it actually works:
- Stages can occur **simultaneously** (prototyping during research)
- Teams often **loop back** (testing reveals new empathy needs)
- Multiple **iterations** through each phase
- The process is **cyclical**, not sequential

### Practical implications:
- Don't wait for "perfect" completion of one stage
- Expect to revisit early assumptions
- Build prototypes early to learn faster
- Let user feedback guide the journey

As Don Norman emphasizes: "We need more design doing." The framework is scaffolding, not a rigid prescription.

---

## Key Principles Across All Methods

1. **Human-centered**: Start with users, not technology or business goals
2. **Embrace ambiguity**: Comfort with uncertainty enables discovery
3. **Iterate, iterate, iterate**: Learn through cycles of making and testing
4. **Bias toward action**: Build to think; prototype early
5. **Radical collaboration**: Diverse perspectives strengthen solutions
6. **Show don't tell**: Tangible artifacts communicate better than descriptions
