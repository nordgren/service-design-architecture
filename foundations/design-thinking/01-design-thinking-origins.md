# Design Thinking Origins

*Research compiled: 2026-02-14*

## Overview

Design thinking is a human-centered approach to innovation that draws from the designer's toolkit to integrate the needs of people, the possibilities of technology, and the requirements for business success. It has evolved from academic roots in design cognition to become a widely adopted methodology for tackling complex, "wicked" problems.

---

## Historical Timeline

### Pre-1960s: Foundations
- Design thinking has roots in the study of design cognition and design methods dating to the 1950s and '60s
- Also referred to as "designerly ways of knowing, thinking and acting"

### 1969: Herbert Simon's "The Sciences of the Artificial"
**Key Figure:** Herbert A. Simon (1916–2001)
- Nobel Prize-winning economist and cognitive scientist at Carnegie Mellon
- Pioneer in artificial intelligence, bounded rationality, and decision-making
- His seminal work outlined a **7-stage design process**: Define → Research → Ideate → Prototype → Choose → Implement → Learn
- This became the cornerstone of modern design processes

### 1970s: Wicked Problems
**Key Figure:** Horst Rittel
- Introduced the concept of "wicked problems" in the context of design and planning
- With Melvin Webber, contrasted wicked problems with "tame" problems
- **Wicked problems** have no definitive formulation, no true/false solution, and wide discrepancy between perspectives
- Called for a "second generation" of design methods that would be participatory and argumentative

### 1971-1977: Foundational Texts
- **Don Koberg** (1971): *The Universal Traveler: A Soft-Systems Guide to Creativity, Problem-Solving*
- **Robert McKim** (1972): *Experiences in Visual Thinking*
- **George Nelson** (1977): *How to See: A Guide to Reading Our Man-Made Environment*

### 1978: IDEO Founded
- Human-centered design practice from the start
- Would later become instrumental in popularizing design thinking

### 1992: Richard Buchanan
- Framed design thinking through the notion of "wicked problems" in academic discourse
- Connected Rittel's work to broader design theory

### Late 1990s: IDEO's DeepDive™
- ABC Nightline feature demonstrated IDEO's rapid innovation methodology
- **DeepDive™ stages**: Understand → Observe → Visualize → Evaluate → Implement
- Rights later acquired by Deloitte Consulting (2006)

### 2003: Stanford d.school Founded
- **Hasso Plattner Institute of Design** at Stanford University
- **Key Figures:** David Kelley (IDEO founder), Hasso Plattner (SAP co-founder)
- Initially taught via a 3-step process: Understand → Improve → Apply
- Evolved into the famous **5-stage model**

### 2000s-2010s: Mainstream Adoption
- Design thinking became "common parlance" across industries
- Significant growth in applying design thinking to business, education, and social innovation
- IDEO released Design Kit: Human-Centered Design Toolkit for developing countries

## Key Frameworks

### Herbert Simon's 7-Stage Process (1969)
1. Define
2. Research
3. Ideate
4. Prototype
5. Choose
6. Implement
7. Learn

### Stanford d.school 5-Stage Model
1. **Empathize** — Deep focus on humans involved
2. **Define** — Reframe the problem
3. **Ideate** — Divergent thinking, judgment-free idea generation
4. **Prototype** — Make ideas tangible
5. **Test** — Validate and iterate

*Note: Represented as hexagons to emphasize non-linear, iterative nature*

### IDEO 3-Stage Model
1. **Inspire** — Problem/opportunity identification
2. **Ideate** — Synthesis and solution generation
3. **Implement** — Concrete action planning

### Design Council Double Diamond (Mid-2000s)
Based on Béla H. Bánáthy's 1996 "divergence-convergence" model:
1. **Discover** — User needs identification (diverge)
2. **Define** — Align needs with objectives (converge)
3. **Develop** — Design-led solution iteration (diverge)
4. **Deliver** — Finalize and launch (converge)

## Core Principles

### Common Traits Across All Frameworks
- **Starts with empathy** — Deep focus on human needs
- **Reframes problems** — Gain new perspectives
- **Divergent then convergent thinking** — Generate many ideas, then focus
- **Prototyping and testing** — Make ideas tangible
- **Iteration** — Revisit and refine throughout
- **Collaborative, multidisciplinary teams**

### Cognitive Features
- Deals with ill-defined and "wicked" problems
- Solution-focused strategies (vs. problem-focused)
- Abductive/productive reasoning (inference from available information)
- Co-evolution of problem and solution
- Non-verbal, graphic/spatial modeling (sketches, prototypes)

### AIGA's "Head, Heart, Hand"
- **Head** — Cognitive problem-solving
- **Heart** — Empathy and understanding emotions
- **Hand** — Practical creation and making

## Key Concepts

### Wicked Problems
Problems that are complex, open-ended, and ambiguous:
- No definitive formulation
- No "right" or "wrong" solution
- Wide discrepancy between different perspectives
- Cannot be solved with rules or technical knowledge alone

### Solution-Focused vs. Problem-Focused
Bryan Lawson's research found architects (designers) employ **solution-focused** cognitive strategies, while scientists employ **problem-focused** strategies. Designers use "solution conjectures as the means of developing their understanding of the problem."

### Divergent & Convergent Thinking
- **Divergent:** Open exploration, generating many possibilities
- **Convergent:** Focusing, selecting, refining the best options

### Co-evolution
Designer's attention oscillates between understanding the problem and generating solutions — each informs the other iteratively.

## Critiques & Evolution

### Concerns
- Term "design thinking" can be used superficially without true commitment
- Hasn't always retained consistent meaning as it spread
- Some criticize it as a "panacea" when applied without depth

### IDEO's Perspective
"Applying design thinking with integrity means continuing to deepen and refine—to be lifelong learners and practitioners at the same time."
