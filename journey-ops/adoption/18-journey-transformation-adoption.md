# Journey Transformation Adoption

## Overview

Transforming an organization to become journey-centric represents one of the most significant operating model shifts in modern customer experience management. Unlike tactical CX initiatives, journey transformation requires fundamental changes in mindset, structure, governance, and culture across the entire organization. This transformation typically unfolds over multiple years and demands sustained executive commitment, cross-functional collaboration, and deliberate change management.

**Key Insight:** Journey-centric transformation is not a project—it's an organizational evolution that requires changing how people think, work, and measure success across every level of the enterprise.

---

## The Journey-Centric Operating Model

### What Makes It Different?

Journey-centric organizations fundamentally differ from traditional product-centric or channel-centric models:

| Dimension | Product-Centric | Journey-Centric |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Mindset & Focus** | Features and needs within a product | Customer needs, goals, and experiences across journey touchpoints |
| **Goal** | Improve products to enable interactions and increase revenue | Improve customer and business outcomes; create seamless, cohesive experiences |
| **Collaboration Style** | Product teams and business functions working independently in silos | Cross-functional journey teams collaborating across departments |
| **Priorities** | Competing departmental priorities and budgets | Business-driven shared priorities across journey and product teams |
| **Accountability** | Channel/product owners | Shared accountability for journey performance |

## Forrester's Three-Phase Transformation Framework

Research from Forrester identifies three distinct phases organizations must navigate to achieve journey centricity, each requiring movement across six operational levers.

### Phase 1: Activate

**Objective:** Build initial momentum and success stories

**Key Activities:**
- Identify executive sponsor and secure leadership commitment
- Build a "strawman" journey atlas to map the organization's journey landscape
- Prioritize "lighthouse journeys" - high-impact journeys that can demonstrate quick wins
- Establish pilot journey teams to build success stories
- Begin socializing the journey-centric vision

**Typical Duration:** 6-12 months

### Phase 2: Connect

**Objective:** Establish deeper organizational alignment and embedded practices

**Key Activities:**
- Establish executive alignment across the C-suite
- Embed a universal journey framework more deeply in the organization
- Create cross-functional committees and governance structures
- Develop journey management playbooks and resource libraries
- Standardize journey-mapping and measurement practices
- Expand beyond pilot teams to multiple journey initiatives

**Typical Duration:** 12-24 months

### Phase 3: Extend

**Objective:** Scale journey centricity across the enterprise ecosystem

**Key Activities:**
- Create a single senior-level journey prioritization forum that informs corporate planning
- Establish integrated permanent journey teams
- Implement journey benchmarking and predictive analytics
- Drive advanced hypothesis testing and continuous optimization
- Extend practices into the broader CX ecosystem (partners, suppliers, distributors)
- Orchestrate journeys at scale using advanced technology

**Typical Duration:** Ongoing/continuous improvement

### The Six Operational Levers

Journey-centric transformation requires moving six operational levers **in concert**. Organizations must address imbalances where some levers are more advanced than others:

1. **Strategy & Vision** - Clear articulation of journey-centric principles and business case
2. **Structure** - Cross-functional journey teams, roles, and reporting relationships
3. **Process** - Standardized methodologies, playbooks, and operating rhythms
4. **Technology** - Journey analytics platforms, orchestration tools, and data infrastructure
5. **Governance** - Prioritization forums, decision rights, and accountability frameworks
6. **Measurement** - Journey KPIs, customer feedback systems, and performance dashboards

**Common Imbalances:**
- Strong CX teams and committees (structure) but inadequate technology to analyze journey insights
- Investment in journey-mapping software before cross-functional teams exist to operationalize insights
- Advanced analytics capabilities without governance structures to drive action

## TheyDo's Five-Stage Maturity Model

The journey toward customer-centricity follows a predictable maturity curve. Understanding where your organization sits helps identify the next right steps.

### Level 1: Intuition

**Characteristics:**
- No global system for documenting customer feedback or measuring satisfaction
- Customer journey is not documented
- Handful of innovators working customer-centric in isolation
- Leadership not convinced of need for customer voice in decision-making
- No formalized processes or budgets

**Focus for Advancement:** Build awareness and make the business case

### Level 2: Fragmented

**Characteristics:**
- Customer-centric initiatives starting to show impact
- Results occurring sporadically, in silos
- Customer behavior tracked in CRM but not utilized fully
- Teams not sharing insights (Sales → CS → Product disconnect)
- Mid-level managers see need for change

**Focus for Advancement:** Connect the dots; create shared language and processes

### Level 3: Coordinated

**Characteristics:**
- Customer-first becoming normal beyond Support/Success/Sales
- Product, Marketing, Engineering incorporating customer insights
- Budgets and processes formalized
- Cross-functional collaboration emerging
- Initial journey mapping exercises completed

**Focus for Advancement:** Standardize practices and scale coordination

### Level 4: Scalable

**Characteristics:**
- Business strategy and goals revolve around customer needs
- Dedicated team overseeing customer journey management
- Journey management processes documented and shared in onboarding
- Processes refined regularly
- Customer feedback incorporated into every product decision
- Flywheel for gathering feedback and transforming to actionable insights
- NPS measured and used to guide decisions

**Focus for Advancement:** Deepen embedding and extend into ecosystem

### Level 5: Excellence

**Characteristics:**
- Customer-centricity woven into organizational fabric
- Customer-centric business model identifies opportunities at all touchpoints
- Customer-centric culture where customer wins = personal wins
- Key metrics (CSAT, retention, LTV) consistently high
- Strategy directly informed by customer involvement
- Continuous innovation and optimization

**Focus for Advancement:** Maintain excellence and drive industry leadership

### Six Maturity Dimensions

Assess maturity across these dimensions to identify gaps:

1. **Governance** - How leadership maintains and measures customer-centric standards
2. **Process** - Recurring operations to include customer at all development stages
3. **Culture** - How teams think and speak about customers
4. **Organization** - Teams and roles dedicated to customer experience
5. **Measurement** - Journey KPIs, data accuracy, and relevance
6. **Tools** - CRMs, journey management platforms, and their effectiveness

## Change Management for Journey Transformation

### The Mindset Shift Challenge

**Critical Principle:** Change mindset before you change operations.

Journey transformation fails when organizations jump to deliverables before securing buy-in. Successful transformations prioritize deliberate change management as much as the work itself.

#### Top-Down Mandate Isn't Enough

Even with C-suite vision and mandate, transformation requires buy-in across the organization—up and down the org chart. Cultural change cannot be decreed; it must be cultivated one step at a time.

### Three Pillars of Change Management

#### 1. Training

Ease the transition by helping people understand the new mindset and their role:

**Effective Approaches:**
- **Informational road shows** - Bringing the vision to different parts of the organization
- **Guest speakers** - External experts lending credibility
- **Formal training programs** - Structured skill-building
- **Resource libraries** - Self-serve learning (strategy docs, playbooks, external articles, case studies)
- **Working groups** - Peer-led education connecting design efforts to business outcomes

**Example:** One organization created a working group to teach designers how to connect design efforts to business outcomes, following established operational processes from project scoping to delivery.

#### 2. Time

**Key Insight:** People need to hear about journey-centric design from multiple sources and acclimate at their own pace before adopting it in daily work.

**Recommendations:**
- Don't rush - start small with pilot efforts
- Expand after a few cycles that include process improvements
- Allocate transition buffers (several months) before requiring participation
- Allow employees time to educate themselves, discuss with colleagues, and build ownership

**What Not to Do:** Underestimate how long transformation takes or what's required to make it happen.

#### 3. Socialization

**Showcase successful journey-design efforts** and explain how journey-centric mindset and operations achieved results.

**Effective Tactics:**
- **Easy-to-use case study templates** - Lower barrier to sharing success stories
- **Forums for showcasing work** - "Design Month" events with activities, speakers, and presentations
- **Emerging leaders programs** - Train future leaders on evangelizing the strategy

**Effective Case Study Template:**
1. Overview of original journey experience and insights
2. Strategy for the journey, including problem statement
3. How cross-functional journey team worked together (roles, milestones, cadences, ceremonies)
4. Outcomes - improved metrics and qualitative feedback themes

## Governance: Balancing Autonomy and Control

### Establish Process Governance

**Critical Asset:** Journey Management Playbook

All successful journey-centric organizations create comprehensive playbooks documenting:
- General approach to journey management
- Methodology and frameworks
- Roles and responsibilities
- Templates and tools
- Best practices and lessons learned

**Playbook Owner Role:**
- Dedicated team member maintaining and improving the playbook
- Ensures consideration of best practices
- Incorporates improvements from active journey teams
- Facilitates knowledge transfer

### Support Trial and Error

**Key Principle:** Nobody gets it right on the first try. Theory must meet reality, and institutional tolerance for change must exist.

**Journey Operations Team:** Two of the organizations in NN/g's research created separate journey-operations teams focused on:
- Onboarding new journey teams
- Documenting unique needs
- Adjusting existing processes situationally
- Removing obstacles from journey managers' day-to-day work
- Managing journey libraries (maps and blueprints)
- Maintaining consistency of outputs
- Selecting and maintaining tools
- Creating efficiencies
- Establishing criteria for journey selection
- Establishing metrics for journey success
- Conducting measurement and reporting

## Cross-Functional Collaboration

### Cooperate, Don't Compete

**Journey Management Mantra:** "Cooperate, don't compete."

#### Avoid Working in Isolation

Customer journeys transcend organizational silos and digital products. Stakeholders from these areas must be actively involved in planning and supporting journey-centric design.

**Challenge:** Journey teams borrow resources from other business areas, potentially creating competition.

**Solution:** Proactive and consistent communication between stakeholders to diffuse competition over resources.

#### Relate Efforts to Business Goals

**Critical Success Factor:** Bridge the gap between design and business leaders through effective communication.

**Case Study: Investment Journey Redesign**

A financial institution formed a journey team to improve the experience of working with investment advisors. Third-party advisors used the institution's tools, but:
- Tool adoption was dismal
- Application dropoff rate was high

**Research Findings:**
- Investors felt confused and struggled to find relevant information
- Digital solutions and processes for advisors were inadequate

**Approach:**
- Created journey maps documenting findings
- Quantified unrealized gains from incomplete applications
- Highlighted these in journey maps
- Projected sales and revenue gains from improvements

**Results:**
- Application dropoff reduced by 12%
- Tool adoption doubled

#### Create Business-Driven Priorities

Traditional product-centric organizations create competition between departments with separate priorities and budgets.

**Journey-Centric Approach:**
- Develop prioritization method based on overarching business objectives
- Define shared priorities for journey teams and product teams
- Product team work includes both product-centric and journey-centric efforts
- All prioritized based on strategy and business priorities
- Creates alignment and fosters cooperation around shared goals

## Stakeholder Engagement in Journey Transformation

### Apply Journey Thinking to Stakeholders

Forward-thinking organizations are applying journey mapping to **stakeholder journeys**, not just customer journeys. This includes:
- Employee journeys
- Partner journeys
- Supplier journeys
- Advisor/channel partner journeys

**Key Insight:** The stakeholder journey (SJ) extends beyond customers to cover any stakeholder's trajectory of role-related touchpoints and activities enacted through stakeholder engagement.

### Stakeholder Journey Approach to Change Management

Organizations are using customer journey techniques to manage change:

**Benefits:**
- Identify and remove friction points within stakeholder journeys
- Accelerate change lifecycle through proactive resistance management
- Increase end-user adoption and long-term sustainment
- Establish key metrics to measure alignment

**Application:** Create an impact and stakeholder analysis as a foundational part of the project:
- Define all stakeholder groups impacted by change
- Outline their roles in the change journey
- Map their journey through the transformation
- Identify friction points and design interventions

## Balancing Present and Future

### Dream Big, Then Build What Makes Sense Now

Journey management must optimize the current state while keeping an eye on the future state.

**Approach:**
- Design an **ideal-state journey map** as reference point
- Work toward ideal state incrementally
- Continually consider new insights
- Reevaluate long-term strategy as you progress

**Challenge:** Determining how big or small to go at any given time requires constant reevaluation of where things stand relative to the future.

### Flexible Planning Framework

**Roadmap Principles from Leading Organizations:**

**Next Quarter (1-3 months):**
- Clear, firm plans
- Specific deliverables and milestones
- High certainty

**Following Quarters (4-6 months):**
- Medium level of certainty
- Flexible priorities that could shift
- Coordination with product teams

**Beyond Six Months:**
- Loose, highly malleable placeholders
- Potential work, subject to change
- Strategic themes rather than specific initiatives

**Critical Element:** Managed through effective communication between design leaders working toward unified design strategy.

## Measuring Journey Transformation Adoption

### Adoption Metrics Framework

**Organizational Level:**
- Percentage of business units with dedicated journey teams
- Journey centricity maturity scores across six dimensions
- Leadership engagement in journey prioritization forums
- Budget allocation to journey initiatives vs. traditional projects

**Operational Level:**
- Number of active journey teams
- Journey management playbook utilization rate
- Cross-functional team participation rates
- Time-to-market for journey improvements

**Cultural Level:**
- Employee awareness of journey-centric principles (survey)
- Use of journey language in meetings and documentation
- Internal case study creation and sharing rates
- Journey-related training completion rates

**Business Impact Level:**
- Journey-specific KPIs (completion rates, satisfaction, effort)
- Attribution of business outcomes to journey improvements
- ROI of journey transformation initiatives
- Competitive differentiation in journey experiences

### Digital Transformation Maturity Models

Several models exist for assessing digital and journey transformation maturity:

**Common Maturity Levels:**
1. **Intention/Reactive** - Sporadic efforts, no formal approach
2. **Beginner/Defined** - Initial processes and tools in place
3. **Adopter/Integrated** - Coordinated cross-functional efforts
4. **Performer/Optimized** - Scaled, measured, data-driven
5. **Transformer/Innovating** - Industry-leading, continuous innovation

**Assessment Approach:**
- Conduct periodic reassessment (annually or biannually)
- Track progress over time (e.g., "basic" to "advanced" in specific capabilities)
- Measure whether interventions are working
- Use both self-assessment and external validation for objectivity

## Success Factors and Common Pitfalls

### Critical Success Factors

1. **Executive Sponsorship** - Active, visible, sustained leadership support
2. **Dedicated Resources** - Journey operations teams, journey managers, playbook owners
3. **Patience and Long-Term Commitment** - Recognize transformation takes years
4. **Balance of Governance and Flexibility** - Process rigor with room for adaptation
5. **Business Language** - Connecting journey improvements to business outcomes
6. **Culture Change Priority** - Treating change management as important as the work itself
7. **Cross-Functional Collaboration** - Breaking down silos through shared priorities
8. **Measurement and Celebration** - Tracking progress and showcasing wins

### Common Pitfalls

1. **Tools Before Teams** - Investing in technology before organizational readiness
2. **Rushing the Mindset Shift** - Jumping to deliverables before securing buy-in
3. **Top-Down Only** - Mandate without bottom-up engagement and ownership
4. **Competing Priorities** - Allowing journey and product teams to compete for resources
5. **Lack of Governance** - No playbooks, standards, or shared processes
6. **Short-Term Thinking** - Expecting quick results from multi-year transformation
7. **Isolation** - Journey teams working without connection to business strategy
8. **Forgetting It's Never Done** - Treating transformation as a project with an end date
